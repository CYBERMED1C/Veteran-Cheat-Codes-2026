Updated for **2026**.

> **Important:** This guide is informational only. VA rules, state benefits, rates, and eligibility rules can change. Always verify using VA.gov, your state veterans agency, or a VA-accredited representative before making decisions.

# Start Here

Veteran benefits usually fall into a few major categories:

| Benefit Type | What It Helps With |
|---|---|
| VA Disability Compensation | Monthly tax-free payments for service-connected conditions |
| VA Health Care | Medical care, prescriptions, mental health, specialty care, urgent care, and more |
| Education Benefits | GI Bill, VR&E, scholarships, and dependent education benefits |
| Housing Benefits | VA home loans, funding fee waivers, adapted housing grants |
| Pension | Needs-based monthly payment for certain wartime veterans |
| Burial Benefits | Burial allowances, national cemetery burial, headstones, markers, and flags |
| Employment Benefits | Federal hiring preference, special hiring authorities, job help |
| Family Benefits | CHAMPVA, Chapter 35 DEA, survivor benefits, and dependent compensation |

The biggest benefit unlocks usually happen at:

| Rating | Major Unlock |
|---|---|
| 0% | Service connection established |
| 10% | Monthly compensation begins |
| 30% | Dependent pay begins |
| 50% | Priority Group 1 VA health care; no VA prescription copays |
| 60% | Possible TDIU threshold if one disability is rated 60% |
| 70% | Possible TDIU threshold if combined 70% with one disability at 40% |
| 100% | Maximum schedular disability compensation |
| 100% P&T | Major dependent benefits such as CHAMPVA and Chapter 35 DEA |

# VA Disability Rating Cheat Sheet

A VA disability rating is the percentage VA assigns to a service-connected condition based on severity.

VA ratings are not simply added together. VA uses a “whole person” method.

Example:

| Ratings | VA Math Result |
|---|---|
| 50% + 30% | 65%, rounded to 70% |
| 60% + 40% | 76%, rounded to 80% |
| 70% + 20% + 10% | About 78%, rounded to 80% |

A **0% rating still matters**. It usually does not pay monthly compensation, but it officially establishes service connection. That can help with future increases, secondary claims, treatment access, and evidence history.

# 2026 VA Disability Compensation Rates

These are the base monthly rates for a veteran alone with no dependents.

Rates are effective December 1, 2025.

| Combined Rating | Monthly Compensation |
|---|---:|
| 0% | $0.00 |
| 10% | $180.42 |
| 20% | $356.66 |
| 30% | $552.47 |
| 40% | $795.84 |
| 50% | $1,132.90 |
| 60% | $1,435.02 |
| 70% | $1,808.45 |
| 80% | $2,102.15 |
| 90% | $2,362.30 |
| 100% | $3,938.58 |

## Dependent Pay

VA does not pay extra for dependents at 10% or 20%.

Dependent pay begins at **30%**.

Dependents may include:

- Spouse
- Children
- Dependent parents
- School-age children
- Spouse needing Aid and Attendance

# Benefits by Rating Level

## 0% Rating

A 0% rating means VA agrees the condition is service-connected, but the condition is not severe enough for monthly compensation.

Possible benefits:

- Service connection established
- Possible VA treatment for the service-connected condition
- Foundation for future increase claims
- Foundation for secondary claims
- Useful evidence history for worsening conditions

Example:

A veteran gets 0% for migraines. Years later, the migraines become more frequent and severe. The veteran may file for an increase because service connection has already been established.

---

## 10% Rating

At 10%, monthly disability compensation begins.

Possible benefits:

- Monthly tax-free compensation
- VA health care eligibility
- Possible travel reimbursement for VA care
- Federal 10-point veterans preference
- No VA inpatient or outpatient copays for many categories of care
- Possible VR&E eligibility if employment is affected

---

## 20% Rating

At 20%, compensation increases but dependent pay still does not begin.

Possible benefits:

- Monthly tax-free compensation
- VA health care eligibility
- Federal hiring preference
- Possible VR&E eligibility
- Possible travel reimbursement
- Stronger disability documentation for future claims

---

## 30% Rating

At 30%, dependent pay begins.

Possible benefits:

- Monthly tax-free compensation
- Extra pay for eligible dependents
- VA health care eligibility
- Federal hiring preference
- Possible special hiring authority for 30% or more disabled veterans
- Possible VR&E eligibility
- Possible state benefits depending on location

This is one of the first major benefit unlock levels.

---

## 40% Rating

At 40%, compensation increases and dependent pay continues.

Possible benefits:

- Monthly tax-free compensation
- Dependent pay
- VA health care eligibility
- Federal hiring preference
- Possible special hiring authority
- Possible VR&E eligibility
- Potentially stronger position for future increases or TDIU if conditions worsen

---

## 50% Rating

At 50%, VA health care benefits become much stronger.

Possible benefits:

- Monthly tax-free compensation
- Dependent pay
- Priority Group 1 VA health care
- No VA prescription medication copays
- No VA inpatient or outpatient copays
- Possible concurrent retirement/disability rules for certain military retirees
- Possible state benefits depending on location

This is a major health care threshold.

---

## 60% Rating

At 60%, TDIU may become possible if one service-connected disability prevents substantially gainful employment.

Possible benefits:

- Monthly tax-free compensation
- Dependent pay
- Priority Group 1 VA health care
- No VA prescription copays
- Possible TDIU if one disability is rated 60%
- Possible state benefits depending on location

---

## 70% Rating

At 70%, TDIU may become possible if the veteran has a combined 70% rating with at least one disability rated 40%.

Possible benefits:

- Monthly tax-free compensation
- Dependent pay
- Priority Group 1 VA health care
- No VA prescription copays
- Possible TDIU
- Stronger eligibility for certain state benefits
- Possible caregiver, housing, or employment-related programs depending on facts

---

## 80% Rating

At 80%, the veteran receives high monthly compensation but is still not schedular 100%.

Possible benefits:

- Monthly tax-free compensation
- Dependent pay
- Priority Group 1 VA health care
- No VA prescription copays
- Possible TDIU
- Possible state-level disabled veteran benefits
- Stronger evidence base for future increase claims

---

## 90% Rating

At 90%, the veteran is close to 100%, but VA math makes the final jump difficult.

Possible benefits:

- Monthly tax-free compensation
- Dependent pay
- Priority Group 1 VA health care
- No VA prescription copays
- Possible TDIU
- Possible state-level disabled veteran benefits

Important note:

Going from 90% to 100% can require significant additional disability because of VA math. A veteran may need a new rating or increase that mathematically pushes the combined rating to 95% or higher, which then rounds to 100%.

---

## 100% Rating

At 100%, the veteran receives the maximum schedular disability compensation.

Possible benefits:

- Maximum monthly tax-free compensation
- Dependent pay
- Priority Group 1 VA health care
- No VA prescription copays
- Possible VA dental care
- Possible state-level property tax benefits
- Possible license plate and registration benefits
- Possible hunting/fishing license benefits
- Possible education benefits for dependents if rated permanent and total
- Possible CHAMPVA for dependents if rated permanent and total

---

# 100% Disabled Veteran Benefits

A 100% rating can be schedular or based on TDIU.

Possible 100% benefits include:

| Benefit | Notes |
|---|---|
| Maximum VA Compensation | Highest standard monthly disability payment |
| VA Health Care Priority Group 1 | Broadest VA health care access |
| No VA Prescription Copays | Applies to Priority Group 1 |
| VA Dental Care | Generally available for 100% or TDIU veterans |
| Dependents Pay | Extra pay for spouse, children, dependent parents |
| Commissary and Exchange Access | Disabled veterans may qualify for installation access privileges |
| VA Funding Fee Waiver | VA funding fee may be waived for eligible disabled veterans |
| State Benefits | Varies by state |
| Property Tax Relief | Varies heavily by state |
| License Plate Benefits | Varies by state |
| Education Benefits for Dependents | Usually requires permanent and total status |

---

# 100% Permanent and Total Benefits

100% P&T means VA considers the veteran totally disabled and the condition is not expected to improve.

This is one of the most powerful statuses in the VA system.

Possible 100% P&T benefits:

| Benefit | Who It Helps |
|---|---|
| Chapter 35 DEA | Spouse and children |
| CHAMPVA | Spouse and children if otherwise eligible |
| State Property Tax Exemptions | Veteran or surviving spouse, depending on state |
| Dependents Education Benefits | Eligible dependents |
| Survivor Benefits | May help surviving spouse/family depending on facts |
| VA Dental | Veteran |
| Stronger State Benefit Eligibility | Veteran and sometimes family |

Important:

100% and 100% P&T are not always the same thing.

A veteran may be rated 100% temporarily, schedular 100%, or TDIU. Some family benefits usually require the rating to be **permanent and total**.

# Special Monthly Compensation

Special Monthly Compensation, or SMC, is extra compensation paid in certain serious disability situations.

Examples of situations that may qualify:

- Loss of use of a hand or foot
- Loss of use of reproductive organ
- Aid and Attendance needs
- Housebound status
- 100% rating plus additional separate 60% rating
- Certain severe combinations of disabilities

Common examples:

| SMC Type | Basic Idea |
|---|---|
| SMC-K | Loss of use of a body part or function |
| SMC-S | Housebound or 100% plus separate 60% |
| Aid and Attendance | Needs help with activities of daily living |

SMC can be complex. Veterans should review rating decisions carefully and consider help from a VA-accredited representative.

# VA Health Care

VA health care can include:

- Primary care
- Specialty care
- Mental health care
- Prescriptions
- Prosthetics
- Preventive care
- Urgent care
- Emergency care
- Community care in some cases
- Rehabilitation
- Long-term care in some circumstances

## VA Health Care Priority Groups

| Priority Group | Common Eligibility |
|---|---|
| Group 1 | 50% or higher service-connected, TDIU, Medal of Honor |
| Group 2 | 30% or 40% service-connected |
| Group 3 | 10% or 20% service-connected, Purple Heart, former POW, other categories |
| Group 4 | Aid and Attendance or Housebound benefits |
| Group 5 | Low income, pension, Medicaid |
| Group 6 | Certain 0% compensable, toxic exposure, combat, radiation, Camp Lejeune, other categories |
| Group 7 | Income below certain limits |
| Group 8 | Higher income and other eligibility rules |

## VA Copay Notes

General rules:

- Veterans rated 10% or more usually do not pay inpatient or outpatient copays for many categories of care.
- Veterans rated 50% or more are usually Priority Group 1.
- Priority Group 1 veterans do not pay VA prescription copays.
- Lower priority groups may have medication copays depending on category and condition.

## Urgent Care

VA urgent care in the community may be available if:

- You are enrolled in VA health care
- You received VA care or VA-authorized community care within the required timeframe
- You use an eligible urgent care provider

For emergencies, seek emergency care immediately. VA generally requires timely notification for community emergency care.

# VA Dental Care

VA dental care is much more limited than regular VA medical care.

Veterans may qualify for VA dental care if they:

- Have a service-connected dental condition
- Are rated 100%
- Are paid at the 100% rate due to TDIU
- Are a former POW
- Are in certain VA rehabilitation or treatment programs
- Meet another specific dental eligibility category

Veterans who do not qualify for VA dental care may be able to purchase dental insurance through VADIP.

# VA Education Benefits

Major VA education programs include:

| Program | Who It Helps |
|---|---|
| Post-9/11 GI Bill | Veterans and service members |
| Montgomery GI Bill Active Duty | Veterans with qualifying service |
| Montgomery GI Bill Selected Reserve | Reservists and Guard members |
| VR&E | Disabled veterans with employment barriers |
| Chapter 35 DEA | Dependents of certain disabled or deceased veterans |
| Fry Scholarship | Dependents of certain service members who died in the line of duty |

## Post-9/11 GI Bill

The Post-9/11 GI Bill may pay for:

- Tuition and fees
- Monthly housing allowance
- Books and supplies
- Certain licensing and certification tests
- Some national exams
- Yellow Ribbon benefits at participating schools

For the 2025–2026 academic year, private school tuition is capped at a yearly maximum. Public school tuition may be covered up to the in-state rate if eligible.

## Rudisill / Perkins GI Bill Update

Some veterans with eligibility under more than one GI Bill program may qualify for up to 48 months of combined education entitlement depending on their service history and benefit usage.

Veterans with multiple qualifying active-duty periods should check VA education guidance.

# VR&E Benefits

VR&E stands for Veteran Readiness and Employment.

It used to be commonly called Voc Rehab.

VR&E can help disabled veterans prepare for, find, and keep suitable employment.

Possible VR&E services:

- Career counseling
- Education or training
- Resume help
- Job placement help
- Apprenticeships
- On-the-job training
- Independent living services
- Employment accommodations
- Professional certifications
- Degree programs when approved

Basic eligibility usually requires:

- A qualifying discharge
- At least a 10% service-connected disability rating
- An employment handicap or serious employment handicap

Important:

VR&E is not simply “free college.” It is an employment program. The goal is suitable employment or independent living, depending on the track.

# Dependent and Family Benefits

Dependents may qualify for certain benefits depending on the veteran’s rating and status.

## Dependent Compensation

Extra dependent pay begins at 30%.

Possible dependents:

- Spouse
- Children
- Dependent parents
- School-age children
- Spouse needing Aid and Attendance

## CHAMPVA

CHAMPVA is a health care program for eligible spouses and children.

It may apply if the veteran is:

- Rated permanently and totally disabled due to service-connected disability
- Deceased from a service-connected disability
- Deceased while rated permanently and totally disabled
- Otherwise qualifying under CHAMPVA rules

CHAMPVA is not TRICARE.

## Chapter 35 DEA

Chapter 35 Dependents’ Educational Assistance can help eligible dependents with education and training.

It may apply if the veteran:

- Is permanently and totally disabled due to service-connected disability
- Died from a service-connected disability
- Died while permanently and totally disabled
- Is missing in action, captured, or detained under qualifying rules

Important 2026 note:

VA has announced changes affecting Chapter 35 secondary-school payments for programs starting on or after August 1, 2026. Dependents should verify current rules before planning.

---

# VA Home Loan Benefits

VA home loan benefits can help eligible veterans buy, build, repair, retain, or adapt a home.

Possible advantages:

- No down payment in many cases
- No monthly private mortgage insurance
- Competitive interest rates
- Limited closing costs
- Reusable benefit
- No VA loan limit with full entitlement, subject to lender approval
- VA funding fee waiver for eligible disabled veterans

## VA Funding Fee Waiver

The VA funding fee may be waived for veterans who receive VA disability compensation.

A veteran may also be eligible for a refund if compensation is awarded later with an effective date before the loan closing date.

## Certificate of Eligibility

A VA Certificate of Eligibility, or COE, shows a lender that the veteran meets service requirements for the VA loan benefit.

A COE can usually be requested:

- Online through VA.gov
- Through a lender
- By mail using VA Form 26-1880

# Disabled Veteran Housing Grants

VA adapted housing grants help certain disabled veterans buy, build, or modify a home.

Major grants include:

| Grant | Purpose |
|---|---|
| SAH | Specially Adapted Housing |
| SHA | Special Home Adaptation |
| TRA | Temporary Residence Adaptation |

These grants are usually for veterans with serious service-connected disabilities affecting mobility, vision, burns, respiratory function, or other qualifying conditions.

Examples of modifications:

- Wheelchair ramps
- Wider doorways
- Roll-in showers
- Accessible bathrooms
- Lowered counters
- Improved entryways
- Other disability-related adaptations

# Veterans Pension

Veterans Pension is different from VA disability compensation.

Pension is needs-based.

It may help wartime veterans who:

- Have qualifying wartime service
- Meet income and net worth limits
- Are age 65 or older, or
- Are permanently and totally disabled, or
- Receive SSDI or SSI, or
- Are in a nursing home for long-term care because of disability

Pension may increase if the veteran qualifies for:

- Aid and Attendance
- Housebound benefits

Important:

VA Pension is based on income and net worth. Unreimbursed medical expenses may reduce countable income.

# Federal Employment Benefits

Veterans may qualify for federal hiring advantages.

Possible benefits:

- Veterans preference
- 5-point preference
- 10-point preference
- 30% or more disabled veteran hiring authority
- Veterans Recruitment Appointment
- Schedule A if separately eligible
- Disabled Veterans Outreach Program help
- Local Veterans Employment Representative support

## 30% or More Disabled Veteran Hiring Authority

Veterans rated 30% or more disabled may qualify for a special federal hiring authority.

This can allow agencies to appoint eligible veterans noncompetitively in certain situations.

This does not guarantee a job, but it can be a major advantage.

# Social Security and VA Disability

VA disability and Social Security disability are separate systems.

## SSDI

SSDI and VA disability compensation do not offset each other.

A veteran may receive both if separately eligible.

A 100% VA rating does not automatically guarantee SSDI.

## SSI

SSI is income-based.

VA compensation can count as unearned income for SSI purposes.

## Expedited SSA Processing

SSA may expedite disability claims for:

- Wounded warriors
- Veterans rated 100% permanent and total by VA

# Presumptive Conditions

Presumptive conditions can make VA claims easier.

A presumptive condition means VA presumes the condition is connected to certain service locations, exposures, events, or time periods.

The veteran usually still needs:

- A current diagnosis
- Qualifying service
- Evidence that the condition meets VA criteria

## Common Presumptive Categories

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| PACT Act / Toxic Exposure | Burn pits, particulate matter, toxic substances |
| Agent Orange / Herbicides | Vietnam, certain Thailand locations, other qualifying areas |
| Camp Lejeune Water | Certain conditions after qualifying time at Camp Lejeune or MCAS New River |
| Gulf War Illness | Undiagnosed illness, chronic fatigue, fibromyalgia, functional GI disorders |
| Radiation Exposure | Certain cancers and conditions |
| Former POW Conditions | Nutritional, psychiatric, cardiovascular, and cold injury conditions |
| ALS | Presumptive for qualifying veterans |
| Chronic Diseases Within One Year | Hypertension, arthritis, diabetes, ulcers, and other listed conditions |

## PACT Act

The PACT Act expanded benefits and health care access for veterans exposed to burn pits, Agent Orange, and other toxic substances.

Veterans with toxic exposure concerns should check VA’s official PACT Act page and consider filing a claim or supplemental claim if previously denied.

# How to File a VA Disability Claim

## Step 1: File an Intent to File

Use VA Form 21-0966.

This can preserve your effective date while you gather evidence.

Do this before waiting months to collect everything.

## Step 2: Gather Evidence

Strong evidence may include:

- DD-214
- Service treatment records
- VA medical records
- Private medical records
- Deployment records
- Exposure records
- Line of duty records
- Buddy statements
- Personal statements
- Medical nexus opinions
- C&P exam results

## Step 3: File the Claim

Use VA Form 21-526EZ.

You can file:

- Online at VA.gov
- By mail
- With a VA-accredited VSO
- With a VA-accredited attorney or claims agent

## Step 4: Attend C&P Exams

C&P exams are important.

Do not miss them.

Be honest and clear. Explain your symptoms based on your worst regular days, functional limitations, frequency, severity, and impact on work and life.

## Step 5: Review the Decision Letter

Do not only look at the percentage.

Check:

- Conditions granted
- Conditions denied
- Effective dates
- Whether conditions are static
- Whether future exams are scheduled
- Whether dependents were added
- Whether VA found permanent and total status
- Whether special monthly compensation was considered

## Step 6: Appeal or Review if Needed

If you disagree, act within one year when possible to preserve your effective date.

# Appeals and Review Options

If VA denies a claim or gives a lower rating than expected, there are three main review options.

## Supplemental Claim

Use when you have new and relevant evidence.

Examples:

- New diagnosis
- New medical opinion
- New buddy statement
- New private medical records
- New evidence of exposure
- New service records

Form: VA Form 20-0995

## Higher-Level Review

Use when you believe VA made an error based on the evidence already in the file.

No new evidence is allowed.

You can request an informal conference.

Form: VA Form 20-0996

## Board Appeal

Use when you want a Veterans Law Judge to review the case.

Options may include:

- Direct review
- Evidence submission
- Hearing

Form: VA Form 10182

# Common Mistakes

Avoid these:

- Not filing an Intent to File
- Missing C&P exams
- Filing with weak evidence
- Not claiming secondary conditions
- Not adding dependents after 30%
- Thinking 90% plus 10% equals 100%
- Thinking 100% and 100% P&T are always the same
- Paying unaccredited claim companies
- Not reviewing effective dates
- Ignoring denied conditions
- Not appealing within one year
- Not enrolling in VA health care
- Not checking state benefits
- Not checking CHAMPVA or DEA eligibility for dependents
- Not asking about TDIU when unable to work
- Not checking SMC eligibility

## California Disabled Veteran Property Tax Exemption

California offers disabled veteran property tax exemptions for qualifying disabled veterans and surviving spouses.

Eligibility and exemption amounts can depend on:

- Disability rating
- Blindness or loss of use status
- Income
- Ownership
- Occupancy
- Surviving spouse status

Verify with:

- CalVet
- County assessor
- California State Board of Equalization

## California College Fee Waiver for Veteran Dependents

California has a College Fee Waiver program for eligible dependents of qualifying veterans.

This can waive tuition and certain fees at California public colleges and universities.

Verify eligibility through CalVet and the county veterans service office.

## California DVBE

DVBE stands for Disabled Veteran Business Enterprise.

California DVBE certification may help disabled veteran-owned businesses compete for state contracting opportunities.
